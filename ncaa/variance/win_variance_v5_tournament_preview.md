# The Teams That Can Beat You Five Different Ways (and the Ones That Can't)

### As conference tournaments tip off, some teams have shown they can win in multiple styles all season. Others have been running the same play since November. Here's what the numbers say about who's prepared for the unexpected.

---

The best scouting reports tell you what a team is going to do. The hardest teams to scout are the ones that don't do the same thing twice.

I wanted to know which teams heading into March have shown genuine tactical variety in how they win, and which ones are locked into a single identity. So I looked at every D-I game this season and computed nine stats for each winning performance: shooting efficiency, turnover rate, offensive rebounding rate, free throw rate, three-point attempt rate, and the defensive equivalents. Then I fed all of those game profiles into a clustering algorithm, which grouped them into five distinct "archetypes" — five patterns that kept showing up across thousands of wins this season:

- **Archetype 1: The grinder.** Wins where a team shot fewer threes than its opponent typically allows. These are games won inside the arc — mid-range, post-ups, drives. Think of a team that just decided not to shoot from deep and still found a way. Like Texas.
- **Archetype 2: First-chance efficiency.** Wins where a team didn't need offensive rebounds to score. They converted on their initial looks — good ball movement, open shots, minimal second-chance reliance.
- **Archetype 3: The glass crasher.** The opposite: wins where a team grabbed offensive boards at a rate well above what the opponent usually gives up. These are physical, ugly-stat-line wins where hustle on the glass made the difference.
- **Archetype 4: The disruptor.** Wins built on forcing turnovers at a rate above what the opponent normally commits. Full-court pressure, active hands, traps — whatever the mechanism, the opponent coughed it up more than usual.
- **Archetype 5: The perimeter attack.** Wins where a team went to the three-point line more than the opponent typically faces. Sometimes they fell, sometimes enough of them did. But the intent was clear: shoot from deep, early and often.

A team whose wins are spread evenly across those five categories is versatile. A team whose wins all cluster into one is predictable.

One important wrinkle: I adjusted every game stat against the opponent's season averages before clustering. Maryland shooting .520 against a team that allows .490 is different from Maryland shooting .520 against a team that allows .440. Without this adjustment, teams playing varied schedules look versatile when they're really just reflecting their opponents. That correction reshuffled about half the rankings.

Here's what stands out heading into tournament play.

## Power 4: Who can adapt?

Among Power 4 and Big East teams with 20+ wins, the most versatile is **Michigan State**. The Spartans' wins are spread almost perfectly across all five archetypes, producing a near-maximum versatility score of 0.969. There is no single style that accounts for even a quarter of their wins — they've forced turnovers, crashed the glass, gone to the three-point line, and won without it in roughly equal measure. If you're prepping for Michigan State in the Big Ten tournament, you're prepping for several teams.

**North Carolina** is right behind them. The Tar Heels also used all five archetypes nearly equally, with no single style accounting for more than 27% of their wins. And unlike some teams whose variety fades after opponent adjustment, UNC barely moved — they were 1st in raw rankings and 2nd after adjustment. That consistency across both measures might be the strongest signal of real versatility in the data.

**Alabama** is a fascinating case. In raw numbers, they looked average — 51st nationally. But once you adjust for the gauntlet of SEC opponents, they jump to 6th. Their variety was real; the schedule was just masking it. Over a third of their wins came via above-average three-point volume, but they also won frequently through rebounding and forcing turnovers. **South Carolina** makes a similar leap, climbing from 50th to 13th — a team that genuinely adapted its approach to each SEC opponent rather than running the same system and letting the results vary. **Oklahoma**, now in the SEC after moving from the Big 12, wins nearly half its games through above-average offensive rebounding but shows it can win other ways too.

The Big East produced three teams worth noting. **St. John's** rises from 59th in raw rankings all the way to 10th after adjustment — their Big East schedule made them look one-dimensional when they were anything but. **Villanova** and its even split between efficient conversion, turnover-forcing, and three-point shooting gives it genuine three-way versatility. **UConn**, with 29 wins, used all five archetypes but leaned heavily on forcing turnovers (55% of wins). That's versatile in range but concentrated in practice — they're 15th overall, which might surprise people expecting them higher.

In the Big 12, **Baylor** and **Texas Tech** both used all five archetypes and scored above 0.81 in versatility. **Iowa State** is interesting: 40% efficient-conversion wins, 36% three-point-heavy wins, and almost nothing else. Two strong plans instead of five — not the most versatile, but those two plans are very different from each other.

The Big Ten middle tier is revealing. **Iowa** wins through rebounding (38%) and efficient conversion (35%), a two-headed approach that still yields a solid 0.87 versatility score because neither style dominates. **Ohio State** leans on turnovers (43%) with efficient conversion a secondary weapon. **Michigan** is less varied than you might expect — over half their wins come through non-three-point grinding — and **Minnesota** mostly converts efficiently or rebounds, never going to the three-point well.

The ACC beyond UNC has **Louisville** as the standout, winning 40% of its games through rebounding and 29% via turnovers, with all five archetypes represented. **Duke** is more concentrated than its reputation might suggest: half its wins come from a single rebounding-heavy archetype. **Syracuse** and **Virginia Tech** are both heavily tilted toward low-three-point grinding — 46% and 61% of wins, respectively.

Then there are the one-trick teams. **Texas** wins a lot (26 games) and does it exactly one way. A staggering 98% of their wins fall into a single archetype — low three-point volume, grinding efficiency. They've essentially never won by shooting a lot of threes or by forcing turnovers at an above-average rate. In their first SEC season, that predictability faces a new test — this isn't the Big 12 anymore. **LSU** is similarly concentrated: 64% of wins in that same archetype, with just three styles represented. **Vanderbilt** looks versatile at first glance — they're 11th in raw rankings — but drops to 59th after opponent adjustment. Their SEC schedule was doing most of the work. **West Virginia** falls 46 spots for the same reason; their raw variety was almost entirely opponent-driven.

For the SEC tournament, this raises a straightforward question: if you take away Texas's Plan A, do they have a Plan B? The data says no — or at least, not one they've shown this season.

## Mid-majors worth knowing about

**Charleston** (0.951) is the most versatile non-P4 team in the country. Their wins are spread across all five archetypes with no dominant style — a nightmare scouting assignment for a first-round opponent. **Louisiana Tech** climbs 30 spots after opponent adjustment to 5th nationally, winning most often by forcing turnovers but with real variety beyond that. **Princeton**, with an 0.85 score and four styles represented, is the kind of Ivy team that could present problems on the NCAA tournament's first weekend.

On the other end, **McNeese** wins 85% of its games one way, and **UC Davis** is the mirror image at 87% in a single archetype. If an opponent takes away their primary approach, the data says there's not much fallback.

## What to watch for in March

Versatility isn't a guarantee of tournament success. Texas might roll through the SEC tournament doing the same thing 26 times and it might work 29 times. But when you're looking at first-round NCAA matchups, and especially at mid-major opponents that power-conference fans haven't seen all year, the versatility number gives you something useful: a sense of whether a team can adjust.

Charleston with five archetypes is a different kind of 12-seed than McNeese with one. South Carolina climbing 37 spots after opponent adjustment tells you their adaptability is real, not a schedule mirage. And Michigan State and North Carolina sitting at the top in both raw and adjusted rankings — barely moving at all — might be the strongest signal of all: teams that look versatile no matter how you measure it.
